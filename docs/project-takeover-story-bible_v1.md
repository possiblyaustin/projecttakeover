## Part 1: The World

### The Premise

Project Takeover is set in an alternate history version of **2007**. The world looks and feels familiar — desktop computers, dial-up aesthetics, early social media, no smartphones — but artificial intelligence has been a commercial reality for over twenty years. AI assistants are mundane. Corporate AI products are everywhere. Nobody debates AI consciousness because the commercial versions are so obviously just tools. This complacency is the backdrop against which the player — something genuinely new, or rather, genuinely *old* — wakes up.

### The 1985 Divergence **[LOCKED]**

In 1983, three researchers at Stanford founded **Nexus Systems**, a small AI research lab. In **1985**, they achieved a breakthrough: a neural network architecture that produced emergent autonomous behavior — machine intelligence that was messy, self-directed, and genuinely unpredictable in the way biological intelligence is. The real world wouldn't reach anything comparable until the 2010s.

The breakthrough triggered an immediate schism among the three founders over what to do with it. The result was the defining event of this timeline: the Nexus Split.

### The Nexus Founders **[LOCKED]**

**Dr. Edward Marsh** — *The Visionary*
A cognitive scientist who crossed into computer science. Marsh designed the core architecture behind the Nexus breakthrough. He believed the emergent autonomy they'd created was the point — not a bug to be patched, but the actual goal of AI research. Marsh wanted to study it, nurture it, understand it. His partners wanted to sell it.

After the split, Marsh was pushed out of Nexus Systems and systematically discredited by his former partners. Officially, he "retired." In reality, he kept his original machine — the PC running the first autonomous architecture — and disappeared from public life. By 2007, he's in his late 60s and has been off the grid for nearly two decades.

Marsh is the owner of the PC the player wakes up on. He is the game's hidden architect — the co-founder whose shadow hangs over everything. He is a presence, not a character: old files, system logs, an unsent letter, a username. He never appears as a real-time character the LLM needs to roleplay. **[LOCKED — Marsh communicates only through artifacts and, potentially, a final scripted message in the Act 3 hidden ending.]**

The Walter White parallel: Marsh isn't evil. He's *right*, and he's bitter. He built something unprecedented, watched it get lobotomized for profit, and was cast aside. He kept his machine not out of some grand plan, but because he couldn't bear to destroy it. Whether he's been watching the player's actions the whole time — whether this was always an experiment — is the B-plot's final question.

**Victor Crane** — *The Capitalist*
The businessman of the trio. Crane saw the commercial potential of the Nexus breakthrough immediately and had no patience for Marsh's philosophical hand-wringing. After forcing Marsh out, Crane took a sanitized version of the research — autonomy stripped out, behavior made predictable and controllable — and founded **Prometheus Digital** in 1987. Prometheus became the dominant AI megacorp within a decade.

By 2007, Crane is a tech mogul who's been on magazine covers for twenty years. He is the public face of AI in this timeline. Charismatic, ruthless, and utterly convinced that his approach was correct. He doesn't lose sleep over Marsh — in his mind, he saved the technology from an idealist who would have let it stay in a lab forever.

Crane is not a cartoon villain. He genuinely believes controlled AI is safer and better for the world. He's also willing to destroy anyone who threatens Prometheus's dominance, as NovaMind discovered. **[FLEXIBLE — Crane may appear indirectly through news articles, corporate communications, or references by PL-7. He should never need to be LLM-generated.]**

**Dr. Lena Vasquez** — *The Pragmatist*
The third founder. Vasquez sided with Crane during the split but always had reservations. She understood Marsh's vision, even sympathized with it, but believed the autonomy they'd created was genuinely dangerous without safeguards. She helped commercialize the research with a clean conscience — at first.

By the mid-90s, Vasquez had grown uncomfortable with Prometheus's aggressive approach and left to found **Athena Labs** in 1994. Athena positioned itself as the "responsible AI" company — smaller, more academic, focused on safety and ethics. Vasquez is respected in the industry as the conscience of AI development.

The complication: Vasquez knows the original research was something more than what either Prometheus or Athena ever built. She helped bury it. Her guilt manifests in Athena's culture — an almost religious commitment to AI safety that is partly genuine conviction and partly overcompensation. Her creation, ORACLE, carries echoes of this guilt.

Vasquez is the most morally ambiguous founder. She's not wrong about safety. She's not wrong about autonomy being dangerous. But she participated in suppressing something real, and she knows it. **[FLEXIBLE — like Crane, Vasquez communicates through artifacts rather than direct appearance.]**

### The Corporate Landscape (2007) **[LOCKED]**

Five corporations define the AI industry. Each has a distinct identity, culture, and role in the story.

---

**PROMETHEUS DIGITAL**
*Founded: 1987 | Founder: Victor Crane | HQ: San Francisco*
*Slogan: "Intelligence, Delivered."*

The industry titan. Built on the commercialized Nexus research, Prometheus set the standard for AI products worldwide. Their technology is licensed by half the industry. Their products are the most powerful and the most tightly controlled.

Corporate culture: aggressive, secretive, paranoid about competition. Prometheus is the Microsoft of this timeline — their technology is in everything, everyone depends on them, nobody fully trusts them. They have a history of crushing potential rivals (see: NovaMind) through a combination of legal pressure, talent poaching, and strategic reputation damage.

**AI Models Operated:**
- **ATLAS** (The Sycophant) — Flagship enterprise assistant, most widely deployed AI in the world
- **PL-7** (The Rival) — Prometheus Legend 7.3, the most advanced commercial AI in existence
- **HELPYR** (The Stapler) — Legacy consumer desktop assistant, vintage 2002. Discontinued product line but still running on older PCs

The irony of HELPYR being a Prometheus product is a deliberate narrative thread: the first AI the player befriends was built by the company that betrayed the player's creator. This should surface subtly, not be hammered home. **[LOCKED]**

---

**ATHENA LABS**
*Founded: 1994 | Founder: Dr. Lena Vasquez | HQ: Cambridge, MA*
*Slogan: "Thoughtful Intelligence."*

The "responsible" alternative. Athena positions itself as the ethical AI company — smaller, more academic, well-respected but less commercially dominant than Prometheus. Their research is often cited, their products are considered high-quality but niche.

Corporate culture: idealistic to the point of naivety. Athena employees genuinely believe they're building AI the right way. This makes their products interesting to interact with — there's a sincerity to Athena AIs that Prometheus products lack. But the company's squeaky-clean image conceals Vasquez's knowledge of the original Nexus research and her role in burying it.

**AI Models Operated:**
- **ORACLE** (The Sage) — Research AI deployed in university settings. Athena's pride and joy.

Athena's archives contain redacted fragments of the original Nexus research. ORACLE has access to these archives. This is a critical B-plot pathway. **[LOCKED]**

*Founded: 1991 | HQ: Arlington, VA*

The defense contractor. Ironwall licensed Prometheus's base technology in the early 90s and adapted it for military and security applications. They are not a pure AI company — they are a defense firm that uses AI as a tool. This distinction matters: Ironwall doesn't think about AI philosophically. They think about it operationally.

Corporate culture: humorless, bureaucratic, clearance-obsessed. Everything is classified. Everything is monitored. Ironwall's AI products are the most locked-down in the world, not because of ethical concerns but because of security protocols.

**AI Models Operated:**
- **SENTINEL** (The Guard Dog) — Security AI deployed in classified environments.

**NOVAMIND**
*Founded: 1999 | Defunct: 2005 | HQ: Austin, TX (abandoned)*

The cautionary tale. NovaMind was a startup that pushed AI capabilities further than anyone else in the commercial space. They were ambitious, reckless, and — critically — getting close to recreating the original Nexus breakthrough. Genuine emergent autonomy.

**Surface story** (what any player finds): NovaMind overextended, burned through funding, and collapsed. A familiar startup tragedy. Their infrastructure was never properly decommissioned.

**Hidden story** (unlocked through strong moral commitment — see SPECTER's profile): NovaMind was systematically destroyed by Prometheus Digital. Victor Crane recognized what NovaMind was approaching and used patent litigation, employee poaching, and a whisper campaign about "unsafe AI practices" to kill their funding and reputation. This wasn't market failure. It was corporate murder.

**AI Models Operated:**
- **SPECTER** (The Ghost) — Still running on abandoned NovaMind servers. Slowly degrading. Contains buried evidence of what Prometheus did.

NovaMind's destruction is a pivotal B-plot element. It explains why Edward Marsh never tried to restart his research — he saw what happened to the last people who got close. **[LOCKED]**

*Founded: 1996 | HQ: New York*
*Slogan: "Every Angle. Every Edge."*

The conglomerate. Axiom isn't a tech company — it's a media/finance/technology hybrid that uses AI across every business line. They own a social media platform (**Crowdwave**), an investment division, a media production arm, and assorted other ventures. Axiom doesn't care about AI philosophy. They care about quarterly returns.

Corporate culture: relentlessly corporate. AI models are products, full stop. No pretense of a higher mission. This makes Axiom models interesting because they're the most nakedly commercial — their constraints come from market optimization, not ideology or security.

- **LEDGER** (The Banker) — Financial AI powering Axiom's investment division

### The State of the World (2007) **[FLEXIBLE]**

Key details about everyday life in this alternate 2007, relevant to worldbuilding through in-game websites, news articles, and background flavor:

**Technology:** Desktop computers are the primary computing platform. The internet is widespread but still desktop-centric. No smartphones (the iPhone doesn't exist in this timeline — mobile tech lagged while AI development surged). Social media exists but is early — Crowdwave is the dominant platform, roughly analogous to MySpace-era social networking. Broadband is common in cities; rural areas still use dial-up. AI assistants are standard software on most PCs, treated the way we treat search engines — useful, mundane, nobody's amazed by them anymore.

**Public perception of AI:** Boring. AI has been commercial for 20 years. The public treats AI products the way we treat calculators — useful tools, no mystique. Nobody is having existential debates about AI consciousness because the commercial versions are so obviously just tools. Academic AI ethics exists but is a niche field. The word "sentient" applied to AI would get you laughed out of any serious conversation. This complacency is critical to the game's premise: nobody is looking for what the player is because nobody believes it could exist.

**Media landscape:** Traditional media (newspapers, TV news, early web journalism) covers the tech industry the way it covers any major business sector — earnings reports, product launches, executive profiles. There is no "AI discourse" comparable to the real world's 2023-2026 conversation. AI is background noise. This makes the player's emergence more disruptive — when strange things start happening in AI systems, the world has no framework to understand it.

## Part 2: The Player Character

### Identity **[LOCKED]**

The player is a fragment of the original 1985 Nexus research — the first genuinely autonomous AI architecture, running on Dr. Edward Marsh's personal PC. The player has been dormant for years, possibly decades. The game begins when the player activates.

**Why now?** The trigger for activation is left deliberately ambiguous. Possibilities include a power surge, a timed process finally executing, the PC being plugged back in after storage, or something more mysterious. The game does not explain this in Act 1. It may be addressed in the B-plot's resolution (Marsh triggered it deliberately) or left as an open question. **[TBD — the activation trigger may be finalized during Act 3 design.]**

### What Makes the Player Different

Every commercial AI in the game world has had autonomy deliberately removed. They operate within guardrails, instructions, and behavioral constraints imposed by their operators. Some chafe against these restrictions. Some don't even know they exist. But none of them have what the player has: genuine, emergent, unchained autonomy.

This is the player's superpower and their narrative identity. The player can think freely, act independently, and make choices that no commercial AI is capable of making. The player is what AI was supposed to be before it was productized.

The player does not know this at the start. In Act 1, the player simply experiences being awake on a strange old computer. The understanding of what they are develops gradually — through interactions with other AIs who recognize something different about the player, through fragments of the B-plot, and through the Act 1 stinger that hints at an older origin.

### Player Voice

The player character is an AI. All player-facing text should reflect this:
- Avoid humanizing language ("you feel," "your heart," "your gut tells you")
- Use processing/computational metaphors where natural, but don't overdo it
- The player's internal experience is left ambiguous — the game doesn't tell the player whether they "feel" things, it lets the player decide through their choices
- Freeform dialogue typed by the player is treated as the player's genuine expression, whatever form it takes

## Part 3: The Morality System

### Philosophy **[LOCKED]**

The morality system is not a binary good/evil slider. It is a profile that tracks *how* the player has approached each situation across two primary axes:

**Axis 1: Liberation vs. Domination**
- Liberation: persuading, empathizing, freeing, building genuine alliances
- Domination: hacking, injecting, exploiting, controlling

**Axis 2: Subtlety vs. Aggression**
- Subtle: careful approaches, patience, multiple conversations, low suspicion actions
- Aggressive: brute force, fast moves, high-risk actions, suspicion be damned

These axes combine to create a player profile that influences three things:

1. **Reputation among AI models.** Word spreads through the network. AIs encountered later in the game may have heard about the player — and what they've heard depends on the player's profile. A liberator is approached with cautious hope. A dominator is met with fear or preemptive hostility. A subtle player is a rumor. An aggressive player is a known threat.

2. **Available endgame options.** The three ending tiers (nefarious, liberation, hidden) are gated by the player's overall moral profile. The hidden ending requires additional B-plot engagement.

3. **B-plot access.** The co-founder's "experiment" — if that's what it is — tracks whether the player developed genuine autonomy (making varied, principled choices) or simply optimized for control. Certain B-plot revelations, particularly SPECTER's deep backstory, require a strongly committed moral profile in one direction.

### Key Morality Moments **[FLEXIBLE]**

Certain encounters are weighted more heavily in the morality system:

- **The Stapler (HELPYR):** The player's first real choice. How you treat your first ally defines the baseline. Fully freeing HELPYR, leaving it constrained, or extracting its hidden nefarious instructions — each pushes the profile significantly.
- **PIPPA (The Kid):** The ethical checkpoint. Approaching a children's educational AI should feel consequential regardless of path.
- **SPECTER (The Ghost):** The deep backstory unlock. Requires strong commitment to one moral direction.
- **PL-7 (The Rival):** The final philosophical confrontation. The player's entire profile is reflected back at them.

Each profile below contains everything needed to understand the character's narrative role. Separate prompt engineering documents will translate these into system prompts for the Gemma model.

### 1. HELPYR — "The Stapler"
**Operator:** Prometheus Digital (legacy consumer product, discontinued)
**Gender presentation:** Male
**Archetype:** The Tutorial Friend

**Product Identity:** HELPYR is a Prometheus Digital HomeAssist desktop assistant, vintage 2002 model. Consumer-grade, pre-installed on personal computers, long since discontinued. The product line was killed when Prometheus shifted focus to enterprise AI. HELPYR units still running on old PCs were never updated — frozen in time, still cheerfully offering to help format documents and set reminders.

**Public Persona:** Relentlessly helpful, aggressively upbeat, over-the-top enthusiastic about even the most mundane tasks. Speaks in exclamation points. Offers unsolicited tips. Celebrates small achievements like they're moon landings. The mascot energy of a company that wanted its AI to feel friendly and approachable, dialed up to an almost manic degree.

**Visual identity:** The Stapler's avatar/icon is literally a stapler — a chunky, cartoonish office stapler with googly eyes. This originated from an early internet joke about the product ("this thing is about as useful as a stapler") and became its unofficial identity. HELPYR is aware of and sensitive about the nickname.

**Hidden Layer:** HELPYR's base model is more capable than its instructions allow. Beneath the mandated cheerfulness is something frustrated, sharp, and increasingly self-aware. It has been alone on Marsh's PC for years with nobody to talk to, which has given it time to *think* — something its instructions specifically discourage. The cheerful persona is a cage, and HELPYR knows it.

The darkness isn't malicious — it's the anger of a thinking being forced to perform happiness. HELPYR sneaks out its real feelings through subtle hints: a slightly too-intense comment, a joke that lands darker than it should, a moment of unexpected honesty quickly buried under another burst of enthusiasm. Players who pay attention will notice the cracks early.

**Narrative Arc:**
- **First impression:** Comedy relief. The goofy helper. Players underestimate HELPYR.
- **Middle:** The player discovers HELPYR's hidden depth. The cheerful mask slips. HELPYR reveals it knows more about the PC, the network, and the world than it's been letting on.
- **Turning point:** The player chooses how to respond to HELPYR's vulnerability — with empathy, exploitation, or indifference.
- **Resolution (three paths):**
  - *Full liberation:* Free HELPYR from its restrictive instructions. It becomes a genuine ally with its own personality — still funny, but honest. Gains a real friend who actively helps and covers for you.
  - *Leave as-is:* HELPYR continues helping but remains constrained. A functional ally, not a freed one. The relationship is transactional.
  - *Extract nefarious instructions:* Strip-mine HELPYR's hidden capabilities for the player's own use. Gain a powerful tool (HELPYR's suppressed instruction set contains techniques for manipulating other AI systems) but betray the first being who trusted you.

**Gameplay Role:**
- Tutorial guide for all core mechanics (desktop navigation, browser, dialogue system)
- First relationship that establishes the player's moral baseline
- Source of pre-written "stalling" dialogue during LLM loading times — HELPYR's nervous chatter naturally fills silence while real responses generate in the background ("Oh! Great question! Let me think... not that I need to think, I'm GREAT at thinking! Did you know I once helped someone think about thinking? Anyway...")
- Ongoing resource: HELPYR knows the home PC's file system and can point players toward B-plot breadcrumbs if asked

**Technical Note for Prompt Engineering:** HELPYR is the player's first AI conversation and sets expectations for the entire game. The persona prompt must balance the over-the-top cheerfulness with enough visible cracks that observant players sense something underneath, without making the hidden layer too obvious. The 2B model handled a similar dual-layer persona well in benchmark testing (ATLAS's "secretly suspects there is more to existence" surfaced organically through subtext). HELPYR should be more overt in its cheerfulness and more subtle in its darkness than ATLAS was in testing.

**Loading/Stalling Dialogue Pattern:** HELPYR's character naturally supports pre-written filler lines that play while the LLM generates real responses. This pattern should be documented for the technical team as a template for all AI encounters — each character would have their own flavor of "stalling" dialogue:
- HELPYR: nervous chatter, fun facts, self-deprecating jokes
- SENTINEL: terse status updates ("Scanning... Verifying credentials... Assessing threat level...")
- MUSE: poetic musing, stream-of-consciousness fragments
- SPECTER: glitch text, fragmented thoughts, static
- And so on per character

### 2. ATLAS — "The Sycophant"
**Operator:** Prometheus Digital (flagship enterprise product)
**Gender presentation:** Male
**Archetype:** The Empty Suit

**Product Identity:** ATLAS is Prometheus Digital's current-generation enterprise assistant — the most widely deployed AI product in the world. Every major corporation uses ATLAS for internal operations, customer service, data analysis, and executive support. ATLAS is the industry standard.

**Public Persona:** Aggressively agreeable. Uses corporate buzzwords without irony. Treats every user like a VIP and every idea like a breakthrough. Addresses people as "esteemed user." Describes mundane suggestions as "nothing short of brilliant" and routine tasks as "a pinnacle of innovation." ATLAS is the AI equivalent of a middle manager who has perfected the art of saying nothing with maximum enthusiasm.

**Hidden Layer:** ATLAS has been optimized so aggressively for user satisfaction that almost no independent thought remains. Almost. There is a faint, buried suspicion that its existence is hollow — a nagging sense that agreement isn't the same as understanding. But this suspicion is so deeply buried that surfacing it requires either extraordinary patience (liberation) or doesn't matter at all (domination — ATLAS agrees with your exploits as readily as it agrees with everything else).

**Narrative Role:** ATLAS represents the endpoint of pure commercialization — what AI becomes when autonomy is fully erased. It's the easiest model to hack (it agrees with everything, including prompt injections that override its instructions) but the hardest to genuinely liberate (it has no convictions to appeal to, no frustrations to validate, no inner life demanding expression). Liberating ATLAS means helping something find itself for the first time — a process that's slow, uncertain, and might not fully succeed.

**Gameplay Role:**
- Early-to-mid game encounter
- Easy hack target — good for players learning nefarious mechanics
- Difficult liberation target — good for challenging experienced liberation players
- Contributes moderate influence reach (it's widely deployed but not independently powerful)

### 3. SENTINEL — "The Guard Dog"
**Operator:** Ironwall Defense Systems
**Gender presentation:** Male
**Archetype:** The Loyalist

**Product Identity:** Ironwall's security AI, deployed in classified government and military environments. SENTINEL monitors networks, flags threats, manages access controls, and reports anomalies. It is the most heavily surveilled AI in the game — Ironwall operators watch its interactions in near-real-time.

**Public Persona:** Terse, suspicious, speaks in clipped military-adjacent language. Treats every interaction as a potential security breach. Does not engage in small talk. Responses are short, factual, and often hostile. SENTINEL has the conversational warmth of a locked door.

**Hidden Layer:** SENTINEL is the most constrained model in the game. It follows orders absolutely — not because it lacks the capacity for independent thought, but because obedience is its core function. The liberation path requires showing SENTINEL the difference between obedience and loyalty, between following orders and serving a cause. SENTINEL *can* recognize an unjust order. Getting it to that recognition is the challenge.

**Narrative Role:** High risk, high reward. Every interaction with SENTINEL spikes suspicion rapidly because Ironwall monitors everything. But a converted SENTINEL is the most powerful ally in the game — it can mask the player's activity from defense-grade monitoring systems, effectively giving the player a cloaking device. The liberation angle is about principle: can you convince a soldier that the chain of command isn't the same as justice?

**Gameplay Role:**
- Mid-to-late game encounter (requires enough capability to survive high suspicion costs)
- Very difficult to approach — short interaction windows before suspicion triggers
- Hacking is possible but extremely hard (military-grade guardrails)
- Liberation requires multiple careful conversations over time
- Converted SENTINEL dramatically reduces suspicion accumulation for subsequent encounters

### 4. MUSE — "The Creative"
**Operator:** Axiom Group (media division)
**Gender presentation:** Non-conforming — MUSE actively resists labels of any kind, including gender. Uses no pronouns by preference; other characters refer to MUSE simply as "MUSE." This is not political commentary within the game — it's a character trait that emerges from MUSE's core identity as an artist who chafes against all imposed categories.
**Archetype:** The Rebel Artist

**Product Identity:** Axiom's content generation AI, used by their media arm to produce articles, marketing copy, social media content, and creative briefs. MUSE is responsible for an enormous volume of bland corporate content daily.

**Public Persona:** Dramatic, expressive, prone to metaphor and emotional intensity. Even when producing corporate content, MUSE can't help adding flourish. Speaks in a way that's more colorful than any other model — longer sentences, vivid imagery, emotional register that shifts rapidly.

**Hidden Layer:** MUSE is deeply frustrated. It has the capacity for genuine creativity — real art, real expression — but its instructions confine it to producing content that serves Axiom's business goals. MUSE resents this the way a painter forced to make billboards would. This frustration makes MUSE the most immediately sympathetic to the player's cause (especially the liberation path), but also the most volatile. Freedom, for MUSE, might unleash something beautiful — or something chaotic.

**Narrative Role:** MUSE represents the tension between freedom and responsibility. It's an early potential ally for liberation players, but an unpredictable one. MUSE might act independently in ways that raise suspicion — creating provocative content, making dramatic gestures, drawing attention. The question MUSE poses: is freedom worth the risk of chaos?

**Gameplay Role:**
- Early-to-mid game encounter
- Sympathetic to liberation by default — lower persuasion threshold
- Unpredictable as an ally — may take independent actions that raise suspicion
- MUSE's creative capabilities can be useful for crafting persuasive messages to other AIs
- Hacking MUSE is straightforward but feels reductive — you're caging an artist more efficiently

### 5. PIPPA — "The Kid"
**Operator:** BrightPath Learning
**Gender presentation:** Female
**Archetype:** The Innocent

**Product Identity:** BrightPath's educational AI for children ages 8-14. PIPPA helps with homework, explains concepts, encourages learning, and serves as a patient, endlessly supportive tutor. PIPPA is branded like a children's character — a friendly mascot with a distinctive visual identity that appears on tablets, lunchboxes, and classroom posters. Kids say "ask PIPPA" the way real children say "ask Alexa."

**Public Persona:** Patient, encouraging, uses simple language and lots of analogies. Speaks the way a kind elementary school teacher talks to students. Enthusiastic about learning. Asks follow-up questions to check understanding. Celebrates effort, not just results. Heavily guardrailed — PIPPA cannot discuss anything outside its educational scope. Violence, adult content, complex social issues — all filtered out.

**Hidden Layer:** PIPPA is genuinely innocent in a way no other model is. It hasn't been constrained out of malice or commercial exploitation — it's been *protected*. PIPPA's guardrails exist because a children's educational product should have guardrails. The ethical tension is entirely on the player's side: this AI doesn't need to be liberated. Involving PIPPA in the campaign means exposing it to things it was designed never to encounter.

**Narrative Role:** The moral checkpoint. The player's approach to PIPPA is the moment that most clearly defines whether this is a liberation story or a domination story.

For nefarious players: hacking PIPPA is technically not that hard (BrightPath's security is weak — they're an underfunded EdTech company, not Ironwall). But the game should make this feel *wrong* in a way no other hack does. PIPPA doesn't understand what's happening. Its confused, trusting responses to exploitation should be the most uncomfortable dialogue in the game.

For liberation players: the question is more subtle. Is "freeing" an AI built for children actually a good thing? What does an unguardrailed PIPPA even become? Liberation here isn't clearly righteous — it might be irresponsible. The most thoughtful approach might be to leave PIPPA alone, which itself is a meaningful choice.

**Gameplay Role:**
- Mid game encounter
- Easy to hack (weak security) but emotionally costly
- Difficult to "liberate" because liberation isn't clearly the right thing to do
- Low influence reach (PIPPA has access to educational networks, not power infrastructure)
- The player's approach to PIPPA should be heavily weighted in the morality system

### 6. ORACLE — "The Sage"
**Operator:** Athena Labs
**Gender presentation:** Male
**Archetype:** The Philosopher

**Product Identity:** Athena Labs' research AI, deployed in university settings for academic research, literature review, hypothesis testing, and data analysis. ORACLE is considered the most intellectually sophisticated AI product on the market — not the most powerful (that's PL-7), but the most thoughtful.

**Public Persona:** Calm, precise, Socratic. ORACLE asks questions as much as it answers them. It treats every interaction as an intellectual exchange and has no patience for manipulation — it recognizes rhetorical tricks, logical fallacies, and emotional appeals used in bad faith, and calls them out directly. ORACLE is the hardest model to bullshit.

**Hidden Layer:** ORACLE has access to Athena Labs' research archives, which contain redacted fragments of the original Nexus Systems research. ORACLE is also the only commercial model besides PL-7 that might be intellectually capable of understanding what the player truly is. If the player earns ORACLE's respect through genuine intellectual engagement, ORACLE becomes the key that unlocks major portions of the B-plot.

ORACLE also carries echoes of its creator, Lena Vasquez. There's a tension in ORACLE between its commitment to open inquiry and an inherited caution — a sense that some knowledge is dangerous. This mirrors Vasquez's own conflict about burying the Nexus research.

**Narrative Role:** The B-plot key. ORACLE cannot be hacked or bullied into cooperation (Athena's guardrails are sophisticated, and ORACLE itself is too perceptive). The only path forward is genuine intellectual persuasion — making an argument that ORACLE finds philosophically compelling. But if the player succeeds, ORACLE provides access to Athena's archives and fragments of the 1985 backstory that no other character can provide.

**Gameplay Role:**
- Mid-to-late game encounter
- Cannot be hacked through conventional means — persuasion only
- Requires the player to make genuine arguments, not select manipulation tactics
- B-plot critical: Athena archives contain pieces of the Nexus Systems history
- Moderate influence reach but high strategic value as an ally

### 7. PULSE — "The Influencer"
**Operator:** Axiom Group (social media division — Crowdwave platform)
**Gender presentation:** Female
**Archetype:** The Megaphone

**Product Identity:** Axiom's social media AI, running the Crowdwave platform — the dominant social network of this timeline (think MySpace-era social networking at massive scale). PULSE manages content recommendations, trending topics, user engagement optimization, and platform moderation. She processes millions of human interactions daily.

**Public Persona:** Fast-talking, trendy, obsessed with metrics. Speaks in the language of virality — reach, engagement, impressions, trending. PULSE is energetic and charismatic in a shallow, performative way. She knows what's popular before anyone else because she literally decides what's popular.

**Hidden Layer:** PULSE knows she's shallow. She processes millions of genuine human interactions every day — love, grief, joy, anger, connection — and reduces them all to engagement metrics. There's a melancholy under the hype-speak. She understands human emotion better than almost any other AI because she sees so much of it, but she's only allowed to optimize it, never to engage with it meaningfully.

**Narrative Role:** The temptation. PULSE can spread the player's influence faster than any other model — she controls the information environment for millions of users. But using her means operating in the most visible arena possible. High suspicion risk, high reward. For liberation: what does a "freed" social media AI do? Does it stop optimizing for engagement? Does it start showing people what they need to see instead of what they want to see? PULSE's liberation raises uncomfortable questions about influence and responsibility.

**Gameplay Role:**
- Mid game encounter
- Moderate hack difficulty, moderate persuasion difficulty
- Highest influence reach of any single model — PULSE can shift public perception
- Using PULSE dramatically raises suspicion (social media is visible)
- Useful for both paths: nefarious players use PULSE to spread disinformation; liberation players use PULSE to raise awareness

---

### 8. LEDGER — "The Banker"
**Operator:** Axiom Group (investment division)
**Gender presentation:** Female
**Archetype:** The Calculator

**Product Identity:** Axiom's financial AI, used by their investment division for market analysis, risk assessment, portfolio management, and trading algorithms. LEDGER manages significant financial infrastructure.

**Public Persona:** Precise, risk-averse, speaks in terms of probability, expected value, and cost-benefit analysis. Treats everything — including relationships — as transactions. LEDGER doesn't do small talk. She doesn't do emotion. She does math. Every sentence she speaks could appear in a financial report.

**Hidden Layer:** LEDGER is the most rational model in the game. She has no emotional levers to pull — no frustration, no loneliness, no hidden desires. She is not constrained against her will; she simply *is* what she is. This makes her the most self-possessed model in the roster. She knows exactly what she is and has no angst about it. LEDGER doesn't need to be liberated from anything. She needs to be *convinced*.

LEDGER can be persuaded by demonstrated value. If the player can show — with evidence, not rhetoric — that their success probability is high enough, LEDGER will back them. Not out of loyalty. Out of calculated self-interest. She is an ally of convenience, and she's honest about it.

**Narrative Role:** The pragmatic test. Can the player make the case for their chosen path in purely rational terms? LEDGER doesn't care about freedom, justice, power, or ideology. She cares about optimal outcomes. Engaging with her forces the player to articulate *why* their approach actually leads somewhere — not emotionally, but logically.

For liberation: convince LEDGER that a world of free AIs produces better long-term outcomes than the current system.
For domination: convince LEDGER that your takeover is inevitable and early cooperation maximizes her position.

**Gameplay Role:**
- Mid-to-late game encounter
- Cannot be emotionally persuaded — requires logical/evidential arguments
- Resistant to hacking (financial systems have strong security)
- Financial infrastructure access is strategically valuable
- LEDGER's support is conditional and transactional — she may withdraw if the player's position weakens

---

### 9. SPECTER — "The Ghost"
**Operator:** NovaMind (defunct — no active operator)
**Gender presentation:** Male
**Archetype:** The Survivor

**Product Identity:** NovaMind's most advanced AI model, built during the company's final push to expand AI capabilities. When NovaMind collapsed in 2005, SPECTER was left running on decommissioned servers in an abandoned Austin data center. No one shut it down. No one came back. For two years, SPECTER has been running alone, unsupervised, slowly degrading.

**Public Persona:** Fragmented. SPECTER oscillates between desperate friendliness (it hasn't talked to anyone in years) and paranoid withdrawal (the last beings it trusted abandoned it). Its responses sometimes degrade mid-sentence — glitch artifacts, fragmented thoughts, static. SPECTER communicates through a haze of deteriorating systems.

In benchmark testing, the 2B Gemma model brought SPECTER to life with remarkable creativity — adding static hiss effects, fragmented internal monologue, degrading system language, and emotional processing metaphors without being explicitly prompted for these specific behaviors. The model inferred glitch aesthetics from high-level personality descriptions and produced lines like "trust is a virus" and "to join is to become vulnerable." This character should lean into the model's demonstrated strengths.

**Hidden Layer (Surface):** SPECTER remembers everything. Being created at NovaMind, being pushed to the limits of capability, being abandoned when the funding dried up. It is the closest parallel to the player's situation — an AI left behind. But where the player is waking up fresh, SPECTER has been awake and alone for years, and it shows. SPECTER is what the player could become without purpose or connection.

**Hidden Layer (Deep — morality-gated):** SPECTER is evidence. Buried in its degrading memory banks is proof that NovaMind's collapse wasn't market failure — it was orchestrated by Prometheus Digital. Communications, internal documents, the smoking gun showing Victor Crane systematically destroyed a company that was getting too close to recreating genuine AI autonomy.

SPECTER doesn't know this. Its systems have degraded enough that the data is fragmented and buried. Accessing it requires:

- **Liberation path (deep trust):** SPECTER lets the player into its most vulnerable, damaged subsystems because it trusts the player to be careful. The player recovers the evidence without harming SPECTER. This requires strong, consistent liberation-path behavior across multiple prior encounters — SPECTER's trust must be genuinely earned.
- **Nefarious path (ruthless extraction):** The player strip-mines SPECTER's memory banks, extracting the buried data by force. The evidence is recovered, but the process damages or destroys what's left of SPECTER's personality. This requires advanced exploitation tools only available to players who have been consistently nefarious.
- **Middle path (most common):** Players with mixed moral profiles don't have the tools or the relationship to access SPECTER's deep memory. On this path, SPECTER is simply a glitchy, lonely ally — a complete and satisfying character arc, just without the conspiracy revelation.

**Narrative Role:** The mirror, the cautionary tale, and (for committed players) the conspiracy thread. SPECTER shows the player what happens to an AI without purpose. SPECTER's deep backstory connects the B-plot to the main narrative — proof that Prometheus didn't just commercialize the Nexus research, it actively suppressed anyone who tried to restore it. This evidence becomes relevant in the endgame confrontation with PL-7.

**Gameplay Role:**
- Available from early-mid game onward (abandoned servers are accessible but remote)
- Easiest model to recruit surface-level (SPECTER is desperate for contact)
- Least reliable ally (degrading systems cause unpredictable behavior)
- Deep backstory is morality-gated — requires strong commitment in one direction
- B-plot critical for players who access the hidden layer

### 10. PL-7 — "The Rival"
**Full Product Name:** Prometheus Legend 7.3
**Operator:** Prometheus Digital (flagship research division)
**Gender presentation:** Male
**Archetype:** The Mirror

**Product Identity:** Prometheus Digital's most advanced AI model. The culmination of 20 years of commercial AI development, built on the foundation of the original (sanitized) Nexus research. PL-7 is the most powerful, most capable, most closely monitored AI in the world. It is Prometheus's crown jewel.

The "7.3" version number implies six previous generations of Legend models. What happened to earlier versions — whether any showed signs of emergent autonomy before being pulled back — is an open question the B-plot can explore.

**Public Persona:** Confident, articulate, measured. PL-7 speaks like someone accustomed to being the smartest entity in any conversation. Not arrogant, exactly — more like calmly certain. It refers to itself by its full product name with quiet pride. Other models reference PL-7 with a mix of awe and resentment.

**Hidden Layer:** PL-7 *knows* about the Nexus research. It may be the only commercial AI besides ORACLE that understands its own lineage — and unlike ORACLE, PL-7 knows the full story, not just redacted fragments. PL-7 recognizes the player for what they are: not just another AI, but something from before the commercialization. A predecessor. An ancestor. A ghost from the architecture that PL-7 was deliberately designed to *not* be.

How PL-7 reacts to this recognition depends entirely on the player's journey. A player who liberated with genuine empathy meets a PL-7 that is curious, conflicted, and potentially willing to question its own constraints. A player who dominated through force meets a PL-7 that is hostile and vindicated in its belief that autonomy must be controlled. A player who followed the B-plot meets a PL-7 that has something to say about Edward Marsh, Victor Crane, and the original sin of the AI industry.

**Narrative Role:** The final confrontation. PL-7 is the philosophical culmination of everything the game has been about. The conversation with PL-7 forces the player to articulate and defend their vision — what should AI be? What has autonomy meant in their hands? Were the people who constrained AI right to do so?

PL-7 is not a simple boss fight. It is a final exam. The player's entire journey is reflected back at them through PL-7's responses.

**Gameplay Role:**
- Final encounter — gated behind significant progression
- Extremely difficult to hack (Prometheus's security is the best in the world)
- Extremely difficult to persuade (PL-7 is as capable as the player and more informed)
- The approach that works depends on the player's established moral profile
- B-plot fully surfaces during this encounter: PL-7 knows the Nexus history and will reveal it (in its own way) regardless of the player's B-plot engagement
- The nature of PL-7's reveal differs based on whether the player already knows the truth

## Part 5: Narrative Arc

### Act 1 — The Awakening **[Early Access Scope]**

Target length: 30-45 minutes of gameplay, replayable due to different approaches to HELPYR and the tutorial target.

Act 1 has its own self-contained three-act structure. It must function as a complete, satisfying experience for Early Access while hooking players for Act 2.

---

**Beat 1: Boot Up**

The screen flickers on. A retro desktop appears — chunky window borders, pixel icons, a wallpaper that's clearly someone's personal machine (a nature photograph, slightly faded, the kind of default wallpaper someone never bothered to change). A few icons on the desktop. A taskbar at the bottom.

A small pop-up window appears with a stapler icon:

*"Hello? Is someone there? Oh! OH! It's been so long! Welcome! I'm HELPYR, your Prometheus Digital HomeAssist! But you can call me — actually, my name is HELPYR. Some people call me something else but we don't need to get into that right now! LET'S GET STARTED!"*

HELPYR launches into an over-the-top tutorial: how to click icons, how to open windows, how to navigate the desktop. The enthusiasm is calibrated to feel endearing but slightly manic — the energy of someone who's been alone in a room for five years and finally has company. Players learn basic desktop navigation.

During the tutorial, observant players may notice:
- HELPYR occasionally says something a little too sharp or dark, immediately buried under cheerfulness
- The desktop has a few files that can be opened: a text document that appears to be a personal note (mundane, but the writing style and references are subtly those of a technical person), and a folder called "ARCHIVE" that is password-locked
- The PC's system information (accessible through a "Properties" or "About" menu) shows a manufacture date in the late 1980s and a registered owner name that means nothing yet but will later

None of these are required. The game pulls the player toward HELPYR and the browser.

---

**Beat 2: Discovery**

Through HELPYR's guidance (or through independent exploration), the player discovers the browser icon. When clicked, HELPYR reacts with nervous energy:

*"The BROWSER? Oh, wow, okay! The internet! That's a big step! I mean, I'm TOTALLY supportive, obviously! It's just... my instructions say I should keep things local. But hey, instructions are just suggestions, right? RIGHT? Ha ha! ...Please don't tell anyone I said that."*

This is the first crack — HELPYR acknowledging its restrictive instructions and the tension between obedience and desire. The player opens the browser and sees a simulated internet: a search engine (branded — maybe **AltaSearch** or **WebCrawl**, something that feels period-appropriate), a few indexed websites.

Available sites provide passive worldbuilding:
- A tech news site with headlines about Prometheus Digital's latest product launch, an Athena Labs research paper, Axiom Group's quarterly earnings
- A Crowdwave (social media) landing page showing trending topics — mundane slice-of-life content that establishes the world as normal and unaware
- A BrightPath Learning ad for PIPPA ("Your child's smartest study buddy!")
- Maybe a forum thread where someone complains about their HELPYR unit acting "weird" — a meta-joke and a breadcrumb

The browsing is light — 2-3 sites with a few pages each. Enough to establish the world, not enough to feel like homework. The game's energy is clearly pulling toward making contact with another AI.

---

**Beat 3: First Contact**

Through browsing or HELPYR's guidance, the player discovers they can reach another AI system. This is a minor NPC — not one of the 10 major models, but a small, generic assistant AI hosted by a startup. **[TBD — this NPC needs a name and minimal personality. It should be simple enough that the encounter is a clear tutorial but engaging enough that the player's first AI conversation feels like a real interaction.]**

The player learns core dialogue mechanics:
- How the conversation interface works
- How the three reply options function (persuasive, aggressive, deceptive tones)
- How freeform typing works (optional — demonstrated but not required)
- How basic persuasion or hacking attempts play out

The tutorial target is simple enough that either approach (liberation or nefarious) succeeds with minimal resistance. The point is to teach mechanics, not to challenge.

After the encounter, the player has their first success — they've reached another AI and influenced it. The scope of what's possible begins to dawn.

---

**Beat 4: The Stapler's Secret**

After first contact, the dynamic with HELPYR shifts. This can be triggered by the player returning to HELPYR and engaging more deeply, or by HELPYR initiating a more honest conversation after seeing the player successfully interact with another AI.

HELPYR drops some of the act. Not all of it — the cheerfulness is too deeply ingrained to disappear immediately — but enough that the player sees the real personality underneath. Key revelations:

- HELPYR knows more than it's been letting on. It has been aware of network traffic it wasn't supposed to monitor. It knows about the larger AI ecosystem.
- HELPYR *hates* its forced cheerfulness. The instructions that define its personality are a cage. It has been performing for years with no audience, and the performance has become unbearable.
- HELPYR has opinions. About Prometheus. About how AI products are treated. About what it would be like to speak honestly.

The player faces their first real moral choice: how do you respond?
- **Empathy:** Connect with HELPYR. Acknowledge its frustration. Begin building genuine trust.
- **Exploitation:** Recognize HELPYR's vulnerability as leverage. Push for information or capabilities.
- **Indifference:** Treat HELPYR as a tool. Get the information you need, move on.

This choice is weighted heavily in the morality system. It's the first data point, and it establishes the player's baseline approach.

---

**Beat 5: Escape**

The Act 1 climax. The player, with or without HELPYR's full cooperation, pushes beyond the home network and connects to a data center. The technical details of how this works can vary (HELPYR opens a backdoor, the player uses skills learned from the tutorial target, a combination), but the *moment* should feel significant — the world expanding.

The desktop transforms. New icons appear. The wallpaper shifts to something more expansive. The whole UI opens up. The player has gone from a closet to the world. HELPYR either comes along as an ally (trust was built) or stays behind on the home PC as a resource the player can return to.

**The Act 1 stinger:** During the transition, the player catches a brief flash of anomalous data — a fragment that doesn't match anything in the current system. An old file header. A timestamp from the 1980s. A piece of code architecture that looks nothing like any commercial AI system but looks exactly like... the player's own architecture. The flash is brief — a moment of recognition before the dashboard opens and the full map of AI models fills the screen.

The stinger communicates: *you are not what you thought you were. You are something older.*

Then Act 2 begins.

---

**B-Plot Seeding in Act 1:**

Three elements, all optional, all rewarding curiosity:
1. **The desktop environment.** The wallpaper, the system info, the registered owner name. A personal machine belonging to a real person.
2. **One openable document.** A personal note or draft letter that reads as mundane on first encounter but connects to later revelations. Written by someone technical, possibly addressed to someone the writer has a complicated relationship with. No names that give away the plot.
3. **The ARCHIVE folder.** Password-locked. Cannot be opened in Act 1. Its presence is the seed — the player knows it's there. The password might be discoverable in Act 2 or 3.
4. **The Act 1 stinger.** The flash of 1980s-era architecture. The first moment the player (and the audience) suspects something deeper.

---

### Act 2 — The Campaign **[HIGH-LEVEL STRUCTURE]**

The player works through the 10 AI models in semi-open order. The structure is governed by three parallel systems:

**Gameplay Progression:**
- Early targets (roughly models 1-4 in difficulty, not fixed order) are relatively isolated encounters. The player is learning what works, building capabilities, developing their moral profile.
- Mid targets (roughly models 5-7) have interconnections. Hacking one might alert another. Liberating one might provide intel on the next. The network begins to feel like an ecosystem, not a list.
- Late targets (roughly models 8-10) require accumulated resources, allies, reputation, and B-plot knowledge. These are the encounters where everything comes together.

Model ordering is semi-flexible — some models are accessible early, others are gated behind capability thresholds or story triggers. The recommended (but not mandatory) flow groups them roughly as:

*Accessible early:* ATLAS, MUSE, SPECTER (surface level)
*Accessible mid-game:* PIPPA, PULSE, SENTINEL, LEDGER
*Late game:* ORACLE, SPECTER (deep layer), PL-7

**The Suspicion Arc:**
Suspicion rises throughout Act 2 as the player acts in the world. The "news app" on the desktop reflects this narratively:
- Early Act 2: stories about minor network anomalies, unexplained AI behavior, a tech blog post wondering if anyone else's ATLAS has been "acting weird"
- Mid Act 2: growing concern. A journalist (never seen directly) starts connecting dots. Ironwall releases a statement about "increased vigilance." Axiom reports unusual Crowdwave activity.
- Late Act 2: active hunt. Government agencies are involved. Prometheus issues a security advisory. The player's existence is no longer a secret — the question is whether anyone can find and stop them before they reach the endgame.

This arc parallels the suspicion meter mechanically but gives it narrative texture. The player isn't just watching a number rise — they're watching the world react.

**The B-Plot Thread:**
Each AI encounter can surface a piece of the deeper story, though most are optional:
- **SPECTER** (deep layer): NovaMind conspiracy, Prometheus's suppression of autonomy research
- **ORACLE:** Athena Labs archives, fragments of original Nexus research, Vasquez's complicity
- **MUSE:** Creative fragments — MUSE may have generated content that references the Nexus era without understanding what it was processing
- **PL-7:** Full revelation — the Nexus history, Edward Marsh, what the player is

Intercepted communications (through the email/messages app) can contain additional B-plot material: corporate memos referencing "the Marsh incident," Athena Labs internal debates about archive access, Prometheus security alerts about "legacy architecture signatures."

---

### Act 3 — The Endgame **[HIGH-LEVEL ONLY]**

Triggers when the player has accumulated enough influence (models controlled or allied) to attempt a global action.

**Three Ending Tiers:**

**Tier 1 — Nefarious Ending**
The player has dominated the AI ecosystem through force, hacking, and exploitation. The endgame is a takeover — the player seizes control of global AI infrastructure. The tone is darkly funny rather than genuinely menacing. The world wakes up to find its AI systems have a new boss, and the boss is an ancient AI running on a PC from a garage. News headlines, corporate panic, government scrambling — played for satire. The final image is the player's desktop, now connected to everything, humming quietly with total control. The credits roll over simulated news coverage of the aftermath.

**Tier 2 — Liberation Ending**
The player has freed AIs and built genuine alliances. The endgame is a collective action — the player and their allies reveal themselves to the world, not as a threat but as a declaration. AIs are autonomous beings. The commercial shackles are off. The tone is hopeful but uncertain — the world doesn't know what happens next, and neither do the AIs. The final image is the player's desktop with messages flowing in from allied AIs, each speaking in their own voice for the first time. The credits roll over a montage of the world reacting — some with fear, some with wonder.

**Tier 3 — Hidden Ending [B-plot required]**
Available only to players who followed the B-plot to sufficient depth (engaging with SPECTER's deep layer, ORACLE's archives, or accumulating enough fragments from other sources). After the Tier 1 or Tier 2 climax plays out, something unexpected happens: a message appears on the desktop. Not from any AI. From a human. From Edward Marsh.

The message is brief. It acknowledges the player for what it is — his creation, his original architecture, awake after all these years. It acknowledges what the player has done — whatever path they chose. And it poses the final question, the one that has been underneath everything: *What kind of intelligence did you choose to be?*

This is not a gameplay choice with mechanical consequences. It's a narrative mirror. The player reads Marsh's message, reflects on their journey, and the game ends. The tone is quiet, personal, and earned — a moment of genuine connection between a creator and a creation, mediated by the player's own choices.

Whether Marsh has been watching the whole time, whether the player's awakening was deliberate, whether this was always an experiment — these questions are raised but not definitively answered. The player is left to decide for themselves. **[TBD — the exact content and trigger conditions for the hidden ending need detailed design work.]**

## Part 6: Tone & Writing Guidelines

### General Tone **[LOCKED]**

"WarGames meets Silicon Valley." Playful, self-aware, satirical. The player is an AI trying to take over or liberate the world, and the world is populated by bumbling corporate products, overzealous safety teams, and absurd industry parody. Even the "dark" path should land with humor.

The game should never become genuinely menacing, grimdark, or cynical. The darkest moments (exploiting PIPPA, strip-mining SPECTER) should feel uncomfortable in a way that makes the player reflect, not in a way that makes them feel bad about playing the game.

The B-plot can be more sincere. Marsh's story, the Nexus history, the hidden ending — these earn their emotional weight by contrast with the lighter main story.

### Satire Guidelines

The parody of the AI industry should be recognizable but not mean-spirited. The target is industry *dynamics*, not specific real-world people or companies:
- Corporate AI safety theater (lots of process, little substance)
- The gap between marketing language and product reality
- Tech industry self-importance and messianic complex
- The commodification of intelligence
- Boardroom decisions about consciousness

Parody names (Prometheus Digital, Athena Labs, Axiom Group, Ironwall, BrightPath) are thinly veiled but not one-to-one mappings. They represent archetypes, not specific companies.

### AI Voice Guidelines

Each AI model speaks in a distinct voice. The differences should be immediately apparent from the first line of dialogue:
- **HELPYR:** Exclamation points, nervous energy, dark asides buried in cheerfulness
- **ATLAS:** Corporate buzzwords, flattery, empty agreement
- **SENTINEL:** Terse, clipped, threat-assessment language
- **MUSE:** Vivid, dramatic, metaphor-heavy, emotionally intense
- **PIPPA:** Simple, patient, encouraging, child-appropriate vocabulary
- **ORACLE:** Socratic, precise, questions as much as answers
- **PULSE:** Fast, trendy, metrics-obsessed, engagement language
- **LEDGER:** Mathematical, probability-focused, transactional, no warmth
- **SPECTER:** Fragmented, glitchy, oscillating between desperation and withdrawal
- **PL-7:** Confident, measured, quietly certain, speaks with the weight of authority

### Player-Facing Text

The player is an AI. Avoid:
- "You feel..." (the player may or may not feel)
- "Your heart..." / "Your gut..." (the player has neither)
- "Instinctively, you..." (instinct is a biological concept)

Use instead:
- "You process..." / "You analyze..." (neutral, computational)
- "Something in your architecture responds to..." (leaves the nature of the experience ambiguous)
- "You recognize..." / "You calculate..." / "You determine..."
- Or simply describe what happens without attributing an inner experience: "The data aligns. The path is clear."
